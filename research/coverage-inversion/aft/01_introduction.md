# §1 — Introduction

Tokenised equities promise composability as programmable building blocks — lending collateral, AMM assets, structured-product legs — but that value is gated on the on-chain price being reliably pegged to the underlying *at the moment a protocol acts on it*. Off-hours that peg is neither market-priced nor acted upon: median weekend trade density on xStock-backed perps is $0.10\%$ (zero-trade weekends common), and across 102 tokenised-equity liquidations on a \$4B-TVL Solana market over nine months, none fell on a weekend or overnight (101/102 in regular hours). The segment is nonetheless inflecting (~\$963M, $+2{,}878\%$ YoY [coindesk-tokenized-2026]; ~\$16T projected by 2030 [bcg-tokenization-2030]), and extended-hours reform (24X, NYSE 22h, Nasdaq near-24h [sec-24x; nasdaq-24h]) narrows but — being five-day with a maintenance pause — leaves the weekend, the highest-variance closed window, uncovered.

The window is structural: a US equity is open ~6.5 hours per weekday — roughly 32% of wall-clock time — and in the other 68% the price still moves (earnings, macro prints, overseas sessions) while the venues that establish reference prices are closed. The off-hour token process is *dual*: Cong et al. [cong-tokenized-2025] show on one panel that off-hour token returns predict the close-to-open underlying return ($\lambda = 0.903$, adjusted $R^2 = 0.839$) — a real conditional-mean signal — *and* that extreme off-hour prints "likely would have triggered halts in regulated markets," because the circuit breakers absent on token venues are absent regardless of cause. A naive last-trade feed forwards both the signal and the shock; a naive smoother destroys both. Recent losses name the failure mode: the JELLYJELLY incident on Hyperliquid (~\$12M HLP loss) and a tokenised-asset perp episode with \$9.21M of liquidations both propagate *uncertainty present in the input that was not represented in the output*. The structural fix is an oracle whose contract passes the conditional-mean signal through as its centre while bounding shocks inside a coverage-calibrated width — making input uncertainty a first-class output a consumer reads on every block. Any protocol that accepts tokenised RWAs as collateral or as AMM inputs must answer, at every block: *with the underlying venue closed, what is the fair price — and how uncertain is it?*

**No incumbent RWA oracle deployed at scale publishes a calibration receipt verifiable against public data** — a statement of the form *"over $N$ historical windows, our 95% band contained the realised price $N_\text{in}$ times."* Chainlink Data Streams exposes a `marketStatus` flag, Pyth emits `(price, conf)` with a publisher-level (not aggregate) coverage assertion, CF Benchmarks and SEDA emit regulated scalars with no on-wire uncertainty field, and optimistic oracles guarantee eventual correctness, not a distributional statement conditional on time-of-week (§2). The integrity primitive asks whether $\hat P_t \approx P_t^\text{true}$; the *calibration* primitive asks, given the oracle's own band, whether it contains $P_t^\text{true}$ at the claimed rate. An oracle can be integrity-perfect and calibration-meaningless.

We invert the conventional point-plus-confidence interface — **coverage inversion**: target coverage $\tau$ is a published request parameter, the served band is the product, and the served quantile is an auditable per-read receipt. The deployed architecture is a locally-weighted Mondrian split-conformal predictor — per-regime conformal quantiles on per-symbol volatility-standardised residuals, served around a factor-adjusted point (§4). The closed-market window is not only the weekend but every trading night: the same architecture, with only its gap selector changed, calibrates on overnight (close→next-open) gaps as well, so the contribution is calibration-transparency across *closed-market hours* generally. The overnight cadence also surfaces the sharpest, uniquely *schedulable* instance — the earnings night — where, because the release is publicly dated, the band widens deterministically in advance (**calendar-conditioned coverage**, §4.3.1), a property no incumbent exposes.

We evaluate on two closed-market panels over ten US-listed tickers (2014-01-17 → 2026-04-24): 5,996 weekend (Friday 16:00 → Monday 09:30 ET) rows and 22,624 overnight rows, spanning seven equities/ETFs (SPY, QQQ, AAPL, GOOGL, NVDA, TSLA, HOOD), gold (GLD), long treasuries (TLT), and a Bitcoin-proxy equity (MSTR). The contribution is the *primitive* — the artefact, its serving-time lookup, and the audit receipts — together with empirical evidence (§6) that it delivers per-anchor *and* per-symbol calibration on a decade-scale public-data panel, an open-source reference implementation in Python, Rust, and Anchor (§8), and a worked mapping from served coverage to protocol reserve sizing (§8.7).
