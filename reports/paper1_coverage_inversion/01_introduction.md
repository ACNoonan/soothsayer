# §1 — Introduction (draft)

Tokenized equities and other real-world assets (RWAs) on public blockchains trade 24 hours a day, 7 days a week. Their underlying price-discovery venues do not. A US equity listed on NASDAQ has a regular trading session of 6.5 hours per weekday, or roughly 32% of wall-clock time. In the other 68% — nights, weekends, halts, and extended holiday closures — the price continues to change: earnings are released, macro prints land, geopolitical events clear, and overseas sessions trade correlated instruments. But the venues that establish reference prices are closed.

This gap between continuous on-chain demand and discontinuous off-chain price discovery is a structural feature of the tokenized-RWA architecture. It is also where the great majority of adverse selection, undercollateralized liquidations, and protocol-level loss events have historically concentrated. The pattern is concrete and recent: the JELLYJELLY incident on Hyperliquid (March 2025) drove ~$12M of HLP losses through a manipulable single-venue oracle path; the Volcano Exchange post-mortem of a tokenized-asset perp episode documents a single illiquid spot market triggering $9.21M in Hyperliquid liquidations — *exceeding* the Binance liquidation volume that fed the oracle — and identifies the propagation mechanism as "uncertainty present in the input, not represented in the output, with consumers forced to act as if the point was certain." Any decentralized-finance protocol that accepts tokenized RWAs as collateral or as inputs to an automated market maker must resolve a concrete question at every block: *at this on-chain moment, with the underlying venue closed, what is the fair price — and how uncertain is it?*

The same question recurs across the broader RWA wave that is moving on-chain in 2025–2026: tokenized equities (xStocks on Solana, Backed-issued tokens across multiple chains), tokenized treasuries (BlackRock BUIDL, Ondo OUSG; collectively > $5B AUM as of submission), tokenized commodities (Paxos PAXG, forthcoming silver and other metals), and the next wave of tokenized credit and FX. Every asset class with a continuous off-hours information set — futures, volatility indices, spreads, overseas-session prices — inherits the same closed-market-pricing problem and would benefit from the same calibration-transparent risk-reporting primitive. We argue that this primitive is the *missing infrastructure layer* for responsible institutional-scale RWA onboarding, in the same way that block explorers and indexers are missing infrastructure that the ecosystem depends on but does not directly produce. The methodology fits asset classes with continuous off-hours data; it does not fit instruments without one (e.g., illiquid commodities or pure NAV-update real estate). Section 9.8 enumerates the generalization map; `docs/methodology_scope.md` carries the per-class fit/no-fit table for asset classes that fall outside the methodology's domain.

## 1.1 How existing oracles answer, and what they leave unanswered

Five classes of RWA price oracle are deployed at scale today. Each resolves the *what* of the question above; none publishes a *calibration claim verifiable against public data at the aggregate feed level* — a statement of the form *"over $N$ historical prediction windows, our 95% band contained the realised price $N_\text{in}$ times, giving realised coverage $N_\text{in}/N$."*

| Archetype | Representative | What it publishes during the close | What a consumer sees |
|---|---|---|---|
| Stale-hold | Chainlink Data Streams v10 [chainlink-v10] | Last venue trade held forward + 3-value `marketStatus` | Binary stale/live flag; no `bid`, `ask`, or confidence on the wire |
| Synthetic-marker | Chainlink Data Streams v11 [chainlink-v11] | `bid`/`ask`/`mid` with a `.01` suffix on weekend windows (100% incidence on SPYx/QQQx/TSLAx; §6.7.2) | Band edges are generated bookends, not real venue quotes |
| Quote-dispersion CI | Pyth regular `PriceAccount` [pyth-agg; pyth-conf] | Aggregate price + dispersion-derived $\sigma$ across permissioned publishers | $\sigma$ collapses to near-zero at venue close; not asserted as the s.d. of $P_t$ conditional on regime |
| Executable-overnight | Pyth Pro / Blue Ocean [pyth-pro; blueocean-pyth] | Real 24/5 equity book on Sun–Thu 8 PM – 4 AM ET | Does not cover Friday 4 PM – Sunday 8 PM ET; paid enterprise tier; no aggregate calibration claim |
| Undisclosed methodology | RedStone Live [redstone-live]; Chainlink v10 `tokenizedPrice` | Continuous closed-market value; methodology described qualitatively only | No confidence field; no auditable correspondence between band and probability-of-coverage |

Two structural observations that recur across the table. (i) Pyth's documentation [pyth-conf] recommends that *individual publishers* calibrate their submitted CIs to approximately 95% coverage, but this is a per-publisher self-attestation rather than a verifiable property of the aggregate served feed; downstream protocols read the aggregate. (ii) Cong et al. [cong-tokenized-2025] document that off-hour returns on tokenized stocks anticipate, rather than amplify, subsequent Monday opens — so the on-chain SPL xStock token (SPYx, QQQx, MSTRx) is informationally distinct from its underlier in exactly the closed-market window the oracle covers, and feeds that price the underlier ticker rather than the SPL token (most of the table) carry an additional unobserved instrument-mismatch gap. Pyth Pro's Blue Ocean integration is real-coverage progress for the Sun–Thu overnight window without resolving the Friday-evening-to-Sunday-evening weekend that this paper targets. None of the five publishes the rolling dataset from which an aggregate-level calibration claim could be independently reproduced.

## 1.2 Coverage as a first-class API parameter

The design point of this paper is an RWA oracle whose *product contract* is a calibration statement. We invert the usual interface. Instead of publishing a single band and leaving its coverage level implicit, we take a consumer-specified target coverage $\tau \in (0,1)$ as a request parameter and return the band that has *empirically* delivered coverage $\tau$ on a rolling historical sample, stratified by asset and pre-publish regime.

Concretely, for a point estimator $\hat P_{\text{Mon},r}(s)$ trained per regime $r = \rho(\mathcal{F}_t)$ and a per-regime conformal quantile $q_r(\tau)$ trained on the absolute relative residuals on a held-out calibration set, the deployed v2 / M5 architecture serves a band at consumer target $\tau$ via

$$\bigl[\hat P_{\text{Mon},r}(s) \cdot \bigl(1 - c(\tau') \, q_r(\tau')\bigr),\;\hat P_{\text{Mon},r}(s) \cdot \bigl(1 + c(\tau') \, q_r(\tau')\bigr)\bigr],\quad \tau' = \tau + \delta(\tau),$$

where $c(\tau)$ is an OOS-fit multiplicative bump that closes the train-OOS distribution-shift gap, and $\delta(\tau)$ is a walk-forward-fit shift that pushes per-split realised coverage above nominal. The deployed schedule is a Mondrian split-conformal predictor under exchangeability *within* each regime, with $c(\tau)$ + $\delta(\tau)$ as the deployment-tuned conservatism layer that closes the residual non-stationarity in the regime-conditional distribution. Twenty deployment scalars total: 12 trained per-regime quantiles, 4 OOS-fit $c(\tau)$ bumps, 4 walk-forward-fit $\delta(\tau)$ shifts (matching the v1 hybrid Oracle's 4-scalar `BUFFER_BY_TARGET` parameter budget).

Two properties follow from this construction that are absent in the incumbent designs:

1. **Auditability.** The 20 deployment scalars and the per-Friday point estimator $\hat P_{\text{Mon},r}$ are deterministic functions of public data (prices, factor-futures returns, regime-classifier inputs). Any third party with access to those inputs can reconstruct the artefact and verify that a served band corresponds to the published $\tau' = \tau + \delta(\tau)$ at the documented $c(\tau')$ and $q_r(\tau')$.

2. **Served-coverage receipts.** Every oracle read exposes the tuple $(\tau',\, c(\tau'),\, q_r(\tau'),\, r)$ alongside the band. A protocol integrator can log this tuple, backtest the oracle against its own loss function, and challenge the provider if the realised rate drifts.

We are not the first to argue for calibration in financial forecasting — the framework traces to Christoffersen [christoffersen-1998] and has institutional instantiations in SR 11-7 [sr11-7] / SR 26-2 [sr26-2] model-risk management guidance. What is new here is the application to a decentralised oracle with a consumer-facing SLA on realised coverage rather than on point-estimator accuracy.

## 1.3 What this paper shows

We evaluate the coverage-inversion primitive on a panel of 5,996 weekend prediction-window rows (639 weekends × 10 symbols, with HOOD partial post-2021) spanning 2014-01-17 through 2026-04-24: seven liquid US equities and ETFs (SPY, QQQ, AAPL, GOOGL, NVDA, TSLA, HOOD), one leveraged-gold ETF (GLD), one long-duration treasury ETF (TLT), and one Bitcoin-proxy equity (MSTR). Each prediction window runs from Friday 16:00 ET to Monday 09:30 ET, the contiguous non-trading interval that produces the largest expected drift and the most adverse oracle-failure cases.

Our deployed v2 / M5 architecture combines a factor-switchboard point estimate (Friday close scaled by a per-asset-class futures return: ES/NQ/GC/ZN/BTC) with a per-regime conformal quantile fit on the absolute relative residuals, and constructs the band by serving the consumer at a δ-shifted target $\tau' = \tau + \delta(\tau)$ with the OOS-fit multiplicative bump $c(\tau')$ on the trained quantile. The architecture is deliberately simple — twenty deployment scalars total. We do not claim the underlying forecaster is minimum-variance. The contribution of the paper is the *primitive* that surrounds any such forecaster — the deployment artefact, its serving-time lookup, and the audit receipts — together with empirical evidence that this primitive delivers its coverage promise on held-out data.

The headline empirical claims are:

- At consumer target $\tau = 0.95$, the deployed serving configuration on a temporally disjoint 2023+ slice (1,730 rows, 173 weekends) realises **95.0%** coverage, with Kupiec unconditional-coverage test $p$-value of **0.956** (not rejected) and Christoffersen independence $p$-value of **0.912** (not rejected) at a mean half-width of **354.5 bps** — a 20% reduction over the v1 hybrid Oracle on the same slice (block-bootstrap CI excludes zero on width, straddles zero on coverage). The 12 per-regime conformal quantiles are held-out (frozen on the 4{,}266 pre-2023 calibration rows; no information from the 2023+ slice enters them); the four $c(\tau)$ bumps and four $\delta(\tau)$ shifts are *deployment-tuned* on the same OOS slice (matching the v1 architecture's 4-scalar `BUFFER_BY_TARGET` parameter budget). The strongest defensible characterisation of this number is therefore "deployment-calibrated operating result on a held-out conformal quantile", not "purely held-out end-to-end". A six-split expanding-window walk-forward of the same fit-evaluate cycle (§6.4 / §7.2.4) passes Kupiec at every anchor (per-anchor $p$ = 0.43, 0.37, 0.36, 0.32 at $\tau \in \{0.68, 0.85, 0.95, 0.99\}$), which is the empirical evidence that the deployment-tuned schedule is not idiosyncratically overfit to the 2023+ slice. Reporting Kupiec $+$ Christoffersen on a temporally disjoint slice is the model-risk-management standard format that matches the Basel backtesting framework; we adopt it because protocol integrators and their regulators already speak it. We use $\tau = 0.95$ as the paper's primary *oracle-validation* target; we do not claim in this paper that $\tau = 0.95$ is the optimal liquidation-policy operating point for any particular lending protocol. The deployed default for protocol consumption is $\tau = 0.85$, picked on protocol-EL grounds against a Kamino flat ±300 bps benchmark (forward-referenced to Paper 3); at that target realised coverage is $0.850$ with both tests passing.
- At consumer target $\tau = 0.99$, the deployed v2 / M5 band hits the consumer's nominal target (realised $0.990$, Kupiec $p_{uc} = 0.942$) — closing the v1 finite-sample tail ceiling at $0.972$ at the cost of a 22% wider band ($677.5$ vs $522.8$ bps half-width). The trade is bandwidth-for-honesty: a consumer who requests $\tau = 0.99$ now receives a band whose realised coverage matches the request rather than a v1 band labelled "0.99" but realising $0.972$.
- A two-layer ablation isolates which components are load-bearing for the calibration claim. The constant-buffer width-at-coverage stress test (§7.1) rules out the deployable simpler baseline that strips regime structure entirely (train-fit constant buffer fails Kupiec by 5–14pp at $\tau \le 0.95$ on OOS). The Mondrian conformal-by-regime ablation (§7.2) isolates the deployable simpler baseline that *keeps* the regime classifier and replaces the v1 hybrid forecaster machinery (`reports/methodology_history.md`): per-regime conformal quantile + factor-adjusted point + δ-shifted $c(\tau)$ — the deployed v2 / M5 architecture, which is 19–20% narrower than v1 at indistinguishable Kupiec calibration through $\tau \le 0.95$ on the OOS slice.
- The same diagnostics that keep the paper honest also define the v3 roadmap. Berkowitz rejects on M5's walk-forward PITs ($\text{LR} = 173.1$), and DQ at $\tau = 0.95$ rejects ($p = 5.7 \times 10^{-6}$); the rejections share the v1 architecture's diagnosis (the regime classifier is a coarse three-bin index over the joint distribution of weekend conditioning information) and v2 / M5 does not fix them. We treat those findings not as contradictions of the primitive but as evidence about where the v3 gains should come from: full-distribution conformal procedures, finer regime structure, and richer on-chain signals (§10).

These claims are about the oracle interface and its serving-time calibration behavior. They are not a proof that the same target $\tau$ or the same serving policy is the welfare-optimal liquidation rule for a lending protocol; that stronger decision-theoretic question requires an explicit model of portfolio weights, liquidation costs, and protocol semantics, and is deferred to future work.

## 1.4 Contributions and structure

This paper makes four contributions.

**C1. Coverage inversion as an oracle primitive.** We formalise (§3) the calibration-surface construction and its serving-time inversion, and position it as a consumer-facing SLA distinct from existing point-plus-CI interfaces.

**C2. A 12-year Mondrian split-conformal-by-regime calibration on public data.** We describe (§4, §5) a simple, reproducible point estimator and regime labeler whose 20-scalar deployment artefact can be rebuilt by any third party from freely available data. Our artifact includes the per-Friday parquet, the JSON sidecar with the 12 trained quantiles + 4 $c(\tau)$ + 4 $\delta(\tau)$ scalars, and the serving-time Oracle code in both Python and Rust.

**C3. Held-out-quantile, deployment-calibrated Kupiec $+$ Christoffersen validation.** We report (§6) conditional-coverage diagnostics in the form required by institutional model-risk management, on a slice that is temporally disjoint from the conformal quantile's training window. The 12 per-regime conformal quantiles are held-out; the 4 $c(\tau)$ bumps and 4 $\delta(\tau)$ shifts are deployment-tuned on the same slice and walk-forward-stable across six expanding-window splits (§6.4). DQ and Berkowitz disclosures (§6.4.1) narrow the empirical content to per-anchor calibration in the Kupiec + Christoffersen sense rather than full-distribution calibration.

**C4. A two-layer ablation with bootstrap confidence intervals.** We quantify (§7) which components are load-bearing for the calibration claim. The constant-buffer stress test (§7.1) rules out the deployable simpler baseline without regime structure; the Mondrian conformal-by-regime ablation (§7.2) isolates the deployed v2 / M5 architecture as 19–20% narrower than v1 at indistinguishable Kupiec calibration through $\tau \le 0.95$.
