# §1 — Introduction (draft)

Tokenized equities and other real-world assets (RWAs) on public blockchains trade 24 hours a day, 7 days a week. Their underlying price-discovery venues do not. A US equity listed on NASDAQ has a regular trading session of 6.5 hours per weekday, or roughly 32% of wall-clock time. In the other 68% — nights, weekends, halts, and extended holiday closures — the price continues to change: earnings are released, macro prints land, geopolitical events clear, and overseas sessions trade correlated instruments. But the venues that establish reference prices are closed.

This gap between continuous on-chain demand and discontinuous off-chain price discovery is a structural feature of the tokenized-RWA architecture. It is also where the great majority of adverse selection, undercollateralized liquidations, and protocol-level loss events have historically concentrated. The pattern is concrete and recent: the JELLYJELLY incident on Hyperliquid (March 2025) drove ~$12M of HLP losses through a manipulable single-venue oracle path; the Volcano Exchange post-mortem of a tokenized-asset perp episode documents a single illiquid spot market triggering $9.21M in Hyperliquid liquidations — *exceeding* the Binance liquidation volume that fed the oracle — and identifies the propagation mechanism as "uncertainty present in the input, not represented in the output, with consumers forced to act as if the point was certain." Any decentralized-finance protocol that accepts tokenized RWAs as collateral or as inputs to an automated market maker must resolve a concrete question at every block: *at this on-chain moment, with the underlying venue closed, what is the fair price — and how uncertain is it?*

The same question recurs across the broader RWA wave that is moving on-chain in 2025–2026: tokenized equities (xStocks on Solana, Backed-issued tokens across multiple chains), tokenized treasuries (BlackRock BUIDL, Ondo OUSG; collectively > $5B AUM as of submission), tokenized commodities (Paxos PAXG, forthcoming silver and other metals), and the next wave of tokenized credit and FX. Every asset class with a continuous off-hours information set — futures, volatility indices, spreads, overseas-session prices — inherits the same closed-market-pricing problem and would benefit from the same calibration-transparent risk-reporting primitive. We argue that this primitive is the *missing infrastructure layer* for responsible institutional-scale RWA onboarding, in the same way that block explorers and indexers are missing infrastructure that the ecosystem depends on but does not directly produce. The methodology fits asset classes with continuous off-hours data; it does not fit instruments without one (e.g., illiquid commodities or pure NAV-update real estate). Section 10.5 enumerates the generalization map and §10.6 the asset classes that fall outside the methodology's domain.

## 1.1 How existing oracles answer, and what they leave unanswered

Five classes of RWA price oracle are deployed at scale today. Each resolves the *what* of the question above, and each silences the *how uncertain*.

**Stale-hold.** Chainlink Data Streams' v10 Tokenized Asset schema [chainlink-v10] exposes a `price` field that holds the underlying venue's last observed trade and flags it as closed until the venue reopens — `marketStatus = 1` ("Closed") on the coarse 3-value v10 enum `{0 Unknown, 1 Closed, 2 Open}`. The published `price` is held static across the closed interval; the uncertainty signal is binary (stale or live), so a consumer cannot distinguish a two-hour halt from a three-day weekend from a long Presidents' Day weekend ending an earnings week, even though the *expected* drift across those three intervals differs by more than an order of magnitude. The v10 wire format additionally carries no `bid`, `ask`, or confidence field at all, so any consumer reading v10 directly derives a degenerate zero-width band [chainlink-v10]. The same v10 schema also publishes a continuously-updating `tokenizedPrice` field — empirically observed to drift sub-second across weekends — but with only qualitative public methodology disclosure; that field falls under the *undisclosed-methodology* archetype below.

**Synthetic-marker placeholder.** Chainlink's separately-deployed v11 RWA Advanced schema [chainlink-v11] for the same underlyings extends v10 with explicit `bid`/`ask`/`mid`/`last_traded_price` fields and a 6-value `marketStatus` enum distinguishing pre-market, regular, post-market, overnight, and weekend states (`marketStatus = 5`). The marker-aware classifier of `reports/v11_cadence_verification.md` (v2, 2026-04-26) finds that during weekend windows the wire `bid` carries a synthetic `.01` suffix at 100% incidence on three of the four mapped xStock feeds (SPYx, QQQx, TSLAx) — well above the ~1-in-100 rate that would arise by chance — while the fourth (NVDAx, n=1) classifies as a real quote, a documented mixed verdict at this sample size (§6.7.2). The synthetic-marker pattern is structurally distinct from the stale-hold archetype: the on-wire value is a generated bookend with no claimed relationship to the venue's last-known real price, not a held-forward last-trade. Either way the consumer reading v11 sees a band whose edges are not the real venue quotes.

**Quote-dispersion confidence intervals.** Pyth Network's regular Solana `PriceAccount` surface [pyth-agg; pyth-conf] publishes an aggregate of prices from permissioned publishers together with a confidence interval $\sigma$ derived from the dispersion of those quotes. During market hours this $\sigma$ is an aggregation diagnostic — it measures how much publishers disagree. At the moment of venue close (which is the moment a weekend-targeting consumer reads), publishers are still in lockstep, the 3N-vote pool collapses to a tight interval, and $\sigma$ shrinks to a near-zero half-width — empirically every weekend window in our matched-window panel sees the band-edge price miss the subsequent Monday open (§6.7.1). Mechanical widening, if it occurs, happens *after* the close as publishers drop off, not at the read moment. Critically, $\sigma$ is never asserted to be the standard deviation of the $P_t$ distribution *conditional on* time-of-week, elapsed interval, or realised volatility regime. Protocols that read Pyth's $\sigma$ as a probability-of-coverage statement are extrapolating.

**Executable-overnight cross-venue.** Pyth Pro [pyth-pro] (formerly Pyth Lazer) is the customizable-cadence enterprise tier of Pyth, with the Blue Ocean ATS overnight integration as its load-bearing 24/5-equity differentiator. Since September 2025, Pyth has been the exclusive on-chain distributor of Blue Ocean's executable equity book data through end-2026 [blueocean-pyth]: ~$1B nightly volume, ~5,000 actively-traded NMS symbols, real order-book activity (not extrapolation). Structurally this is the closest published incumbent to a real 24/5 equity feed. **The covered window, however, is 8:00 PM – 4:00 AM ET, Sunday through Thursday — Saturday and most of Sunday are not covered.** The combined regular-Pyth + Pyth-Pro coverage map satisfies "24/5" in the trading-week-weeknight sense; it does *not* cover Friday 4 PM ET through Sunday 8 PM ET, the canonical xStock weekend that this paper targets. Pyth Pro is also a paid enterprise tier whose Pythnet-side aggregation inherits regular Pyth's publisher-dispersion methodology and, like the regular surface, does not surface an aggregate-level calibration claim verifiable against public data.

**Undisclosed methodology.** RedStone Live [redstone-live] and several smaller providers offer 24/7 equity feeds whose closed-market value is computed by methods that are not documented to a level that supports reproducing or auditing a coverage claim. RedStone's public REST gateway [`api.redstone.finance/prices`] additionally carries no confidence, dispersion, or sample-count field on the wire, and its current coverage is for the *underlier ticker* (e.g., SPY) rather than the on-chain SPL xStock token (SPYx), so a Solana protocol holding SPYx as collateral reads a related but not contractually-identical instrument; Cong et al. [cong-tokenized-2025] document that off-hour returns on tokenized stocks anticipate rather than amplify subsequent Monday opens, so the on-chain instrument is informationally distinct from its underlier in exactly the closed-market window the oracle covers. Chainlink Data Streams' v10 `tokenizedPrice` field shares the methodology-disclosure archetype on the same empirical observation: continuous off-hours updating with only qualitative public disclosure of the aggregation rule. A consumer cannot reconstruct the value or audit whether the served band corresponds to any particular probability-of-coverage level. The feed is consumed on trust.

None of these five publishes a *calibration claim verifiable against public data at the aggregate feed level*: a statement of the form *"over $N$ historical prediction windows, our 95% band contained the realised price $N_\text{in}$ times, giving realised coverage $N_\text{in}/N$."* Pyth's documentation [pyth-conf] recommends that *individual publishers* calibrate their submitted confidence intervals to approximately 95% coverage, but this is a per-publisher self-attestation rather than a verifiable property of the aggregate served feed; downstream protocols read the aggregate. Pyth Pro's executable-overnight Blue Ocean integration is real-coverage progress for the Sun–Thu overnight window without resolving the weekend question this paper addresses. None of the five publishes the rolling dataset from which an aggregate-level calibration claim could be independently reproduced.

## 1.2 Coverage as a first-class API parameter

The design point of this paper is an RWA oracle whose *product contract* is a calibration statement. We invert the usual interface. Instead of publishing a single band and leaving its coverage level implicit, we take a consumer-specified target coverage $\tau \in (0,1)$ as a request parameter and return the band that has *empirically* delivered coverage $\tau$ on a rolling historical sample, stratified by asset and pre-publish regime.

Concretely, for a base forecaster $f$ producing bands $[L^f_t(s;q),\, U^f_t(s;q)]$ at a grid of claimed quantiles $q$, we build the empirical calibration surface

$$S^f(s, r, q) \;=\; \frac{1}{|\mathcal{T}_{s,r}|} \sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}_{s,r}} \mathbf{1}\!\bigl[\,L^f_t(s;q) \le P_t(s) \le U^f_t(s;q)\,\bigr]$$

over historical prediction windows $\mathcal{T}_{s,r}$ in each (symbol, regime) bucket. At serve time, we invert: the band delivered for a request $(s, t, \tau)$ is the one at the interpolated $q_\text{served} = (S^f)^{-1}(s, \rho(\mathcal{F}_t), \tau)$.

Two properties follow from this construction that are absent in the incumbent designs:

1. **Auditability.** $S^f$ is a deterministic function of public data (prices, implied-vol indices, calendar flags), the choice of base forecaster $f$, and a documented regime labeler $\rho$. Any third party with access to those inputs can reconstruct $S^f$ and verify that a served band corresponds to the published $q_\text{served}$ on the claimed surface.

2. **Served-coverage receipts.** Every oracle read exposes the tuple $(q_\text{served},\, f,\, r)$ alongside the band. A protocol integrator can log this tuple, backtest the oracle against its own loss function, and challenge the provider if the realised rate drifts.

We are not the first to argue for calibration in financial forecasting — the framework traces to Christoffersen [christoffersen-1998] and has institutional instantiations in SR 11-7 [sr11-7] / SR 26-2 [sr26-2] model-risk management guidance. What is new here is the application to a decentralised oracle with a consumer-facing SLA on realised coverage rather than on point-estimator accuracy.

## 1.3 What this paper shows

We evaluate the coverage-inversion primitive on a panel of 5,986 weekend prediction-window rows (638 weekends × 10 symbols, with HOOD partial post-2021) spanning 2014-01-17 through 2026-04-17: seven liquid US equities and ETFs (SPY, QQQ, AAPL, GOOGL, NVDA, TSLA, HOOD), one leveraged-gold ETF (GLD), one long-duration treasury ETF (TLT), and one Bitcoin-proxy equity (MSTR). Each prediction window runs from Friday 16:00 ET to Monday 09:30 ET, the contiguous non-trading interval that produces the largest expected drift and the most adverse oracle-failure cases.

Our base forecaster combines a factor-switchboard point estimate (Friday close scaled by a per-asset-class futures return: ES/NQ/GC/ZN/BTC) with a log-log regression on a per-symbol volatility index (VIX / GVZ / MOVE) for the conditional sigma, and constructs the band by empirical quantiles of the standardised residual. The forecaster is deliberately simple. We do not claim it is minimum-variance. The contribution of the paper is the *primitive* that surrounds any such forecaster — the calibration surface, its inversion, and the audit receipts — together with empirical evidence that this primitive delivers its coverage promise on held-out data.

The headline empirical claims are:

- At consumer target $\tau = 0.95$, the deployed serving configuration on a temporally disjoint 2023+ slice (1,720 rows, 172 weekends) realises **95.0%** coverage, with Kupiec unconditional-coverage test $p$-value of **1.000** (not rejected) and Christoffersen independence $p$-value of **0.485** (not rejected). The calibration surface is held-out (frozen on the pre-2023 calibration set; no parameter, threshold, or grid point is updated using OOS data); the per-anchor empirical buffer schedule of §4.3 is *deployment-tuned* on the same OOS slice. The strongest defensible characterisation of this number is therefore "deployment-calibrated operating result on a held-out surface", not "purely held-out end-to-end". A six-split expanding-window walk-forward (§9.4 / `reports/v1b_walkforward.md`) ratifies that the deployed buffer at $\tau = 0.95$ ($0.020$) lands at the cross-split mean ($0.019$, $\sigma = 0.017$), which is the empirical evidence that the deployment-tuned value is not idiosyncratically overfit to the 2023+ slice. Reporting Kupiec $+$ Christoffersen on a temporally disjoint slice is the model-risk-management standard format that matches the Basel backtesting framework; we adopt it because protocol integrators and their regulators already speak it. We use $\tau = 0.95$ as the paper's primary *oracle-validation* target; we do not claim in this paper that $\tau = 0.95$ is the optimal liquidation-policy operating point for any particular lending protocol. The deployed default for protocol consumption is $\tau = 0.85$, picked on protocol-EL grounds against a Kamino flat ±300 bps benchmark (forward-referenced to Paper 3; see §10.4); at that target realised coverage is $0.855$ with both tests passing.
- An ablation across eight forecaster variants with block-bootstrap 95% confidence intervals isolates the contribution of each component. The factor-switchboard point and empirical-quantile residual quantile together produce a **39.3% (CI [37.3%, 41.3%])** reduction in pooled band half-width versus a stale-Gaussian baseline. The log-log vol regression is regime-localised: it lifts high-volatility coverage by $10.4\text{pp}$ cumulatively at the cost of wider bands, explaining why the serving-layer hybrid policy switches to the stale-Gaussian band in that regime.
- The per-target buffer schedule on the serving path is the OOS coverage workhorse: replacing surface-inversion-only with a per-anchor empirical buffer (deployed values $\{0.68\!\to\!0.045, 0.85\!\to\!0.045, 0.95\!\to\!0.020, 0.99\!\to\!0.010\}$) closes the coverage gap by $+2.9\text{pp}$ (CI [$+2.0$, $+3.8$]) at $\tau = 0.95$. The hybrid serving policy contributes essentially nothing to mean coverage on OOS data but is the *empirically preferred deployment choice* on the evaluated sample, weakly dominating F1-everywhere on both sharpness and the Christoffersen-independence margin (an evaluation diagnostic, not a decision-theoretic optimisation target): the hybrid simultaneously tightens bands ($-2.6\%$ vs F1-everywhere) and improves the Christoffersen margin ($p_{ind} = 0.485$ vs $0.221$), with no observed trade-off.
- The same diagnostics that keep the paper honest also define the V2 roadmap. Berkowitz rejects, so the present system should be read as a per-anchor calibrated interval oracle rather than a full-distribution-calibrated density forecast; DQ rejects, so residual multi-lag conditional structure remains after the current three-state regime labeler and buffer schedule are applied. We treat those findings not as contradictions of the primitive but as evidence about where the next gains should come from: rolling live rebuilds, finer regime structure, and richer on-chain signals (§10).

These claims are about the oracle interface and its serving-time calibration behavior. They are not a proof that the same target $\tau$ or the same serving policy is the welfare-optimal liquidation rule for a lending protocol; that stronger decision-theoretic question requires an explicit model of portfolio weights, liquidation costs, and protocol semantics, and is deferred to future work.

## 1.4 Contributions and structure

This paper makes four contributions.

**C1. Coverage inversion as an oracle primitive.** We formalise (§3) the calibration-surface construction and its serving-time inversion, and position it as a consumer-facing SLA distinct from existing point-plus-CI interfaces.

**C2. A 12-year empirical calibration surface on public data.** We describe (§4, §5) a simple, reproducible forecaster and regime labeler whose calibration surface can be rebuilt by any third party from freely available data. Our artifact includes the persisted surface, the bounds table, and the serving-time Oracle code.

**C3. Held-out-surface, deployment-calibrated Kupiec $+$ Christoffersen validation.** We report (§6) conditional-coverage diagnostics in the form required by institutional model-risk management, on a slice that is temporally disjoint from the surface's training window. The calibration surface is held-out; the per-anchor empirical buffer is deployment-tuned on the same slice and walk-forward-stable across six expanding-window splits (§6.4). DQ and Berkowitz disclosures (§6.4.1) narrow the empirical content to per-anchor calibration in the Kupiec + Christoffersen sense rather than full-distribution calibration.

**C4. An ablation with bootstrap confidence intervals.** We quantify (§7) which components of the forecaster and serving layer are load-bearing for the calibration claim, and which are disclosed for auditability but contribute no measurable signal at our sample size.
