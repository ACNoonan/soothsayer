Tokenized real-world assets on public blockchains trade continuously, but their underlying price-discovery venues do not -- leaving roughly two-thirds of wall-clock time during which on-chain protocols must value collateral against a closed reference market. We invert the conventional point-plus-confidence oracle interface: the target coverage level tau is a published, parametric product input, and the served price band carries a per-read calibration receipt that any third party can reconstruct from public data. A locally-weighted Mondrian split-conformal architecture maps the asset, its pre-publish regime, a per-symbol volatility scale, and tau to a calibrated band around a factor-adjusted point.

We evaluate on two closed-market panels over the same ten US-listed tickers (2014-2026): 5,996 weekend (Friday-close to Monday-open) rows and 22,624 overnight (close to next-open) rows. The headline weekend claim is held out by leave-one-symbol-out cross-validation at tau = 0.95: realized coverage 0.9497 +/- 0.0128, all folds passing Kupiec. On a 40-cell (symbol x tau) grid the architecture passes 40/40 Kupiec, leading the strongest practitioner baseline GARCH-t (31/40). The same architecture, with only its gap selector changed, generalizes from weekends to overnight gaps, establishing calibration-transparency as a property of closed-market hours generally. Because an earnings release is a scheduled, publicly dated event, the band widens deterministically ahead of it -- calendar-conditioned coverage no incumbent oracle exposes. We report an honest residual: pooled dynamic-quantile tests reject, so the bands are per-anchor calibrated rather than full-distribution calibrated, and we turn this into a measurable reserve-guidance threshold. A reference implementation (Python, Rust, Anchor) is open-sourced.
