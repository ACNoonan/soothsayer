# §2 — Related Work

Our contribution sits at the intersection of three literatures that have, to our knowledge, never been brought together in a deployed system: decentralised-oracle design, cross-venue price discovery, and the calibration / conformal-prediction framework for interval forecasts. A fourth — institutional model-risk-management guidance — supplies the disclosure vocabulary we adopt.

## 2.1 Decentralised oracle designs

The deployed oracle landscape is organised around an *integrity* primitive: can the oracle deliver a faithful value on-chain without a trusted intermediary? Chainlink's decentralised-oracle-network architecture [chainlink-2] aggregates off-chain reports from a node-operator committee, with Data Streams [chainlink-streams] providing pull-based delivery and an explicit `marketStatus` flag — shipped in two co-existing schemas, v10 [chainlink-v10] and v11 [chainlink-v11]. Pyth's Solana surface [pyth-agg; pyth-conf] takes a first-party-publisher approach: each permissioned publisher submits a price and confidence interval; the aggregate rule assigns three votes per publisher (price, price ± confidence) before taking the median. Pyth Pro [pyth-pro] layers subscriber-customisable cadence and the Blue Ocean ATS overnight integration; since September 2025 Pyth has been the exclusive on-chain distributor of Blue Ocean's executable book through end-2026 [blueocean-pyth], covering 8:00 PM – 4:00 AM ET Sun–Thu (the canonical xStock weekend remains uncovered). Switchboard [switchboard] generalises aggregation into permissionless queues; RedStone Live [redstone-live] blends institutional feeds with perpetual-market data; optimistic oracles UMA [uma] and Tellor [tellor] replace continuous aggregation with dispute-and-stake.

None of these designs publishes a *calibration claim* on the served value. Per-archetype empirical evidence — Pyth's $\sigma$ reporting low publisher disagreement at venue close; v11's wire `bid` carrying a synthetic `.01`-suffix at 100% incidence on three of four mapped xStocks; v10 carrying no `bid`/`ask`/confidence field — is in §6.5. Pyth's documentation [pyth-conf] asserts ~95% coverage at the *publisher* level, not as a property of the aggregate; Chainlink's `tokenizedPrice` and RedStone Live describe their off-hours rules qualitatively. Optimistic oracles guarantee *eventual* correctness under economic security but do not define a distributional statement conditional on time-of-week or realised volatility.

The academic literature has similarly concentrated on integrity. Angeris and Chitra [angeris-cfmm] prove conditions under which CFMMs are incentive-compatible as price oracles. Eskandari et al.'s SoK [sok-oracles] catalogues oracle-manipulation attacks; the framing is adversarial (*did the attacker corrupt the value?*) rather than statistical (*over $N$ windows, did the served band contain the truth at the claimed rate?*). The BIS bulletin [bis-oracle] frames the tension as decentralisation-versus-efficiency; on-chain consumers operate under adversarial ordering [flashboys-2], amplifying the cost of any systematic band miscalibration.

Our contribution is orthogonal. The integrity primitive asks whether $\hat P_t \approx P_t^{\text{true}}$; the calibration primitive asks, given the oracle's own band, whether it contains $P_t^{\text{true}}$ at the claimed rate. An oracle can be integrity-perfect and calibration-meaningless: Chainlink faithfully reports Friday's 16:00 close on Sunday at 14:00, but that value is not asserted to be a 95%-coverage estimate of Monday's open. We invert the interface so $\tau$ is a request parameter and $q_\text{served}$ is the receipt.

## 2.2 Cross-venue price discovery

The base forecaster underlying our calibration surface is not methodologically novel; its point estimate is a factor-switchboard scaling of Friday's close by a weekend futures return. The choice of futures as the leading venue applies Hasbrouck's information-share methodology [hasbrouck-1995; hasbrouck-2003], which establishes that most price discovery in the S&P 500 and Nasdaq-100 occurs in E-mini futures — the statement we rely on when scaling SPY and QQQ Friday closes by ES and NQ weekend returns. Earlier work [stoll-whaley-1990; mackinlay-ramaswamy-1988] established intraday lead-lag and mispricing-adjustment dynamics in index-futures markets; Gonzalo–Granger [gonzalo-granger] is the conventional complement.

The Friday 16:00 ET to Monday 09:30 ET weekend is the longest contiguous non-trading interval in the US calendar. French [french-1980] documents the Monday-effect, establishing the weekend as a distinct return-generating regime. Barclay and Hendershott [barclay-hendershott-2003] show after-hours price discovery is non-trivial, so a stale-hold feed is systematically miscalibrated; their companion paper [barclay-hendershott-2004] shows after-hours effective spreads are 3–4× regular-hours, reflecting greater adverse selection — exactly the window in which a protocol holding an RWA as collateral is most exposed. For ETFs, Madhavan and Sobczyk [madhavan-sobczyk-2016] characterise creation/redemption-driven discovery, validating our ETF-class instruments within a unified frame.

A newer thread addresses the structural migration motivating this work. Makarov and Schoar [makarov-schoar-2020] document cross-venue arbitrage in cryptocurrency markets; Lehar and Parlour [lehar-parlour-2025] show Ethereum DEXs do not exhibit long-lived arbitrage relative to centralised benchmarks. Cong et al. [cong-tokenized-2025] provide the first large-scale study of tokenised stocks and find off-hour token returns anticipate Monday opens — direct empirical motivation: weekend price discovery for tokenised equities is migrating to crypto-native venues, so a calibrated band conditioned on the on-chain continuous clock becomes a first-class protocol need.

## 2.3 Calibrated and conformal prediction

Kupiec [kupiec-1995] proposed the unconditional-coverage LR test for VaR; Christoffersen [christoffersen-1998] generalised to conditional coverage, decomposing into Kupiec plus an independence test on the hit sequence. The $p_{UC}$ and $p_{\text{ind}}$ columns we report are these tests; we use the pair rather than the joint because they correspond in banking practice to the Basel traffic-light framework [bcbs-backtest-1996]. The density-forecast generalisation [diebold-gunther-tay-1998] evaluates whether PITs are i.i.d. uniform; Berkowitz [berkowitz-2001] refines this to a small-sample-robust joint LR. Our coverage check at fixed $\tau$ is a marginal slice of the PIT framework, adopted because the protocol-consumer SLA is per-$\tau$.

Gneiting, Balabdaoui, and Raftery [gneiting-calibration-2007] state the modern objective: maximise sharpness subject to calibration. This organises our §7 ablation. Allen et al. [allen-tail-2025] formalise *tail calibration*; the v1 $\tau = 0.99$ ceiling was a tail-calibration failure that M5 closes (§6.3, §7.2.2). The tradition traces to Brier [brier-1950].

Our M5 architecture is a Mondrian split-conformal predictor [vovk-mondrian-2003; vovk-book-2005]: per-regime quantiles $q_r(\tau)$ served after a multiplicative OOS-fit $c(\tau)$ and a walk-forward $\delta(\tau)$. Mondrian conformal preserves finite-sample marginal validity within each category under within-category exchangeability. The v3 upgrade replaces the four-anchor schedule with a continuous conformal correction over the residual CDF — Romano-Patterson-Candès CQR [romano-cqr-2019] is the closest match. For non-exchangeability, Barber et al. [barber-nexcp-2023] extend via weighted quantiles, and Xu-Xie [xu-xie-2021] develop EnbPI for time-series.

## 2.4 Institutional model risk management

SR Letter 11-7 [sr11-7] (Fed/OCC, 2011) articulates a framework for model development, validation, effective challenge, and ongoing monitoring. Any bank integrating an on-chain oracle into a collateral-valuation pipeline operates under this framework — which explicitly requires validation evidence of the form we publish. SR Letter 26-2 [sr26-2], the April 2026 revision superseding SR 11-7 for organisations over $30B, introduces materiality-graduated validation while reaffirming the requirements we target. Basel's 1996 backtesting framework [bcbs-backtest-1996] is the global floor — its mathematical content is the Kupiec test we reproduce.
