# §2 — Related Work (draft)

Our contribution sits at the intersection of three literatures that have, to our knowledge, never been brought together in a deployed system: decentralised-oracle design, cross-venue price discovery, and the calibration / conformal-prediction framework for interval forecasts. A fourth literature — the institutional model-risk-management guidance that governs how regulated firms backtest interval forecasts — supplies the disclosure vocabulary we adopt. We survey each in turn, indicating in each paragraph the specific claim from §1 that the citation supports or the prior design we distinguish against.

## 2.1 Decentralised oracle designs

The deployed oracle landscape on public blockchains is organised around an *integrity* primitive: given a real-world observable, can the oracle deliver a faithful value on-chain without a trusted intermediary? Each of the dominant designs answers this question differently. Chainlink's decentralised-oracle-network architecture [chainlink-2] aggregates off-chain reports from a committee of node operators and delivers a signed report on-chain, with the Data Streams product [chainlink-streams] providing low-latency pull-based delivery and an explicit `marketStatus` flag on real-world-asset feeds. Pyth Network [pyth-agg; pyth-conf] takes a first-party-publisher approach on Solana: each permissioned publisher submits a price and a confidence interval, and the aggregate rule assigns each publisher three votes (price, price ± confidence) before taking the median. Switchboard [switchboard] generalises the aggregation layer into permissionless oracle queues with TEE-attestation, and RedStone's recent Live product [redstone-live] blends institutional feeds with perpetual-market data during off-hours. Optimistic-oracle designs — UMA [uma] and Tellor [tellor] — replace continuous aggregation with a dispute-and-stake mechanism, resolving observable values by economic challenge rather than by committee vote.

None of these designs publishes a *calibration claim* on the served value. Pyth's confidence interval is well-documented [pyth-conf] as an aggregation diagnostic — it measures publisher disagreement and is asserted at the publisher level to be approximately 95% coverage on the spot price, but the aggregate-level interval widens mechanically as contributor sets thin, which is precisely what happens during off-hours for real-world-asset feeds. Chainlink Data Streams' v10 `price` field for tokenized US-equity feeds holds the last observed venue trade and flags it with `marketStatus = 5` until the venue reopens [chainlink-streams]; the served value carries no probability-of-coverage statement, and a consumer cannot distinguish a two-hour halt from a three-day weekend. The same v10 schema's `tokenizedPrice` field updates continuously across the closed interval but does so under undisclosed methodology — empirically observed to drift through several hundred basis points across a single weekend in our V5 tape — and so shares the auditability gap of RedStone's product rather than constituting an alternative archetype. The separately-deployed v11 schema for the same underlyings exposes `bid`/`ask`/`mid` fields that are synthetic placeholders during weekend windows and a `last_traded_price` that is frozen at Friday's close, so on every published v11 field the served value is in the stale-hold class. RedStone's off-hours methodology blends sources in a way that is described qualitatively but not in reproducible form [redstone-live]. Optimistic oracles [uma; tellor] guarantee *eventual* correctness under economic security but do not define a distributional statement conditional on time-of-week or realised volatility. Our claim in §1 that no incumbent oracle publishes a calibration statement verifiable against public data is, on the evidence of these primary sources, not rhetorical: it is the direct reading of the deployed systems.

The academic literature on decentralised oracles has similarly concentrated on integrity. Angeris and Chitra [angeris-cfmm] prove conditions under which constant-function market makers are incentive-compatible as price oracles for on-chain assets, focusing on arbitrage frictions rather than on the distributional properties of the reported series. The SoK by Eskandari et al. [sok-oracles] catalogues oracle-manipulation attacks — flash-loan, liquidity, centralised-feed failures — and surveys mitigations; the framing throughout is adversarial (*did the attacker corrupt the value?*) rather than statistical (*over $N$ windows, did the served band contain the truth at the claimed rate?*). The BIS bulletin on the oracle problem [bis-oracle] frames the tension as a decentralisation-versus-efficiency trade-off and is sceptical of real-world-asset use cases specifically because introducing trusted parties back into the system undermines the trustlessness that is the oracle's whole point. On-chain consumers operate under adversarial ordering [flashboys-2], which amplifies the cost of any systematic band miscalibration.

Our contribution is orthogonal to this prior work. The integrity primitive asks whether the oracle reports $\hat P_t \approx P_t^{\text{true}}$; the calibration primitive asks, given the oracle's own band $[L_t, U_t]$, whether the served band contains $P_t^{\text{true}}$ at the claimed rate on rolling historical evidence. An oracle can be integrity-perfect and calibration-meaningless: Chainlink Data Streams faithfully reports Friday's 16:00 close on Sunday at 14:00, but that value is not asserted to be a 50%- or 95%-coverage estimate of Monday's opening price. We take this observation as the design point, and invert the interface so that $\tau$ is a request parameter and $q_\text{served}$ is the receipt.

## 2.2 Cross-venue price discovery

The base forecaster that underlies our calibration surface is not methodologically novel; its point estimate is a factor-switchboard scaling of Friday's close by a weekend futures return, and the conditional-sigma uses a log-log regression on a regime-matched volatility index. The choice of futures as the leading venue is a direct application of Hasbrouck's information-share methodology [hasbrouck-1995], which decomposes the efficient-price innovation variance across venues of a cross-listed security via a vector-error-correction model. For US equity indices, this methodology [hasbrouck-2003] yields the empirical result that most of the price discovery in the S&P 500 and Nasdaq-100 occurs in the E-mini futures market — the quantitative statement we rely on when we scale SPY and QQQ Friday closes by ES and NQ weekend returns respectively. The Gonzalo–Granger component-share decomposition [gonzalo-granger] is the conventional complement. Earlier foundational results [stoll-whaley-1990; mackinlay-ramaswamy-1988] established the lead-lag in index-futures markets on 5-minute intraday scales and documented mispricing-adjustment dynamics of 15–40 minutes, grounding Hasbrouck's cleaner identification. None of this is ours to claim; our contribution is the primitive that *wraps around* any such forecaster, not the forecaster itself.

The closed-market interval we predict across is specifically the Friday 16:00 ET to Monday 09:30 ET weekend window, which is chosen because it is the longest contiguous non-trading interval in the US equity calendar and, by classical microstructure results, the largest expected-drift window. French [french-1980] documents the Monday-effect — significantly negative mean Monday returns across 1953–1977 — and establishes the weekend as a distinct return-generating regime. Barclay and Hendershott [barclay-hendershott-2003] show that after-hours price discovery, while less efficient per unit time than regular-hours discovery, is non-trivial: information continues to arrive, so a stale-hold feed is systematically miscalibrated with respect to the true $P_t$ distribution. Their companion paper [barclay-hendershott-2004] shows that after-hours effective spreads are 3–4× regular-hours spreads, reflecting greater adverse selection — which is exactly the window in which a protocol holding an RWA as collateral is most exposed to oracle-driven loss events. For ETFs specifically, Madhavan and Sobczyk [madhavan-sobczyk-2016] characterise creation/redemption-driven price discovery and validate that our ETF-class instruments (SPY, QQQ, GLD, TLT) can reasonably be treated within a unified price-discovery frame.

A newer thread of this literature addresses the structural migration that motivates our work. Makarov and Schoar [makarov-schoar-2020] document recurring cross-venue arbitrage in cryptocurrency markets and the role of capital controls in sustaining price deviations. Lehar and Parlour [lehar-parlour-2025] show empirically that decentralised exchanges on Ethereum do not exhibit long-lived arbitrage relative to centralised benchmarks, confirming that on-chain prices carry genuine price-discovery content during venue-active hours. Closing the loop, Cong et al. [cong-tokenized-2025] provide the first large-scale empirical study of tokenised stocks and find that token prices track underlying stocks closely in regular hours but that *off-hour* token returns anticipate — rather than amplify — Monday openings. Their result is the direct empirical motivation for our primitive: weekend price discovery for tokenised equities is actively migrating to crypto-native venues, so a calibrated fair-value band conditioned on the on-chain continuous clock, rather than a stale-hold of Friday's close, becomes a first-class protocol need.

## 2.3 Calibrated and conformal prediction

The evaluation framework we adopt in §6 is not new. Kupiec [kupiec-1995] proposed the unconditional-coverage likelihood-ratio test for VaR systems: given a nominal level $\alpha$ and $N$ out-of-sample observations, the test asks whether the realised hit frequency is statistically consistent with $\alpha$. Christoffersen [christoffersen-1998] generalised this to conditional coverage, decomposing the evaluation into Kupiec's unconditional test and an independence test on the hit sequence, and combining them in a joint likelihood ratio. The $p_{UC}$ and $p_{\text{ind}}$ columns we report in §6 are exactly these two tests; the stated reason for using this pair (rather than the joint statistic alone) is that they correspond in banking practice to the Basel traffic-light framework [bcbs-backtest-1996], which graduates regulatory responses by exception count over a rolling window. The density-forecast generalisation — Diebold, Gunther, and Tay [diebold-gunther-tay-1998] — evaluates whether the PIT series is i.i.d. uniform, and Berkowitz [berkowitz-2001] refines this to a small-sample-robust joint LR test on the inverse-normal-transformed PITs. Our surface-based coverage check at fixed $\tau$ is, in effect, a marginal slice of the PIT framework; we adopt it because the protocol-consumer SLA is per-$\tau$, not distribution-wide.

The modern statement of *what* a good forecast interval is comes from Gneiting, Balabdaoui, and Raftery [gneiting-calibration-2007]: maximise sharpness subject to calibration. The paradigm — calibration is a necessary property of the joint distribution of forecasts and realisations, and sharpness measures the concentration of the forecast — is the framing under which our ablation (§7) is organised: each component of the forecaster either contributes to coverage (calibration) or to bandwidth reduction (sharpness), and we measure each with block-bootstrap confidence intervals. The Gneiting–Katzfuss survey [gneiting-katzfuss-2014] updates this framework for the prediction-space setting. A recent refinement extends the framework specifically to the *tail* of the forecast distribution: Allen, Koh, Segers, and Ziegel [allen-tail-2025] formalise *tail calibration* as a property distinct from full-distribution calibration, with diagnostic tools tied to peaks-over-threshold extreme value theory. This distinction is directly relevant to our setting — a coverage-inversion primitive that is calibrated on average can still be miscalibrated in the tail, which is the failure mode our $\tau = 0.99$ ceiling (§9.1) describes — and we treat their framework as the modern parent literature for the high-$\tau$ behaviour we report. The entire tradition traces to Brier [brier-1950], whose 1950 proper scoring rule for probabilistic weather forecasts is the intellectual origin of this line of work and is the reason that the statistical-forecast-evaluation vocabulary predates the finance literature by several decades.

Our empirical calibration buffer — adding $2.5$pp to the consumer's $\tau$ before inverting the calibration surface — is heuristic. The principled replacement is conformal prediction. Vovk, Gammerman, and Shafer [vovk-book-2005] give the book-length foundation: under exchangeability, split-conformal prediction wraps any base predictor with a calibration-set correction that yields *finite-sample* marginal coverage at level $\tau$. Shafer and Vovk [shafer-vovk-2008] provide the standard tutorial exposition. Romano, Patterson, and Candès [romano-cqr-2019] combine conformal calibration with quantile regression, producing adaptive heteroscedastic intervals with the coverage guarantee preserved — this is the closest methodological match to the conformal upgrade we flag for future work, in which the log-log volatility regression becomes a quantile regressor and the calibration buffer becomes the conformal correction. Two recent developments are critical because our setting is neither exchangeable nor stationary: Barber, Candès, Ramdas, and Tibshirani [barber-nexcp-2023] extend conformal prediction to non-exchangeable data using weighted quantiles and randomisation, establishing coverage guarantees under distribution drift; and Xu and Xie [xu-xie-2021] develop EnbPI, a bootstrap-ensemble conformal interval for dynamic time-series with approximately valid marginal coverage in strongly-mixing settings. Either reference provides a concrete path from our empirical heuristic to a finite-sample guarantee, and we flag both as the upgrade route in §10.

## 2.4 Institutional model risk management

The final literature is not academic but regulatory, and it defines what "institutional-grade calibration disclosure" means. The foundational US supervisory guidance is SR Letter 11-7 [sr11-7], issued jointly by the Federal Reserve and the OCC in 2011, which articulates a framework for model development, implementation, validation, effective challenge, and ongoing monitoring for regulated financial institutions. Any bank integrating an on-chain oracle into a collateral-valuation or capital-computation pipeline is operating under this framework — and the framework explicitly requires validation evidence of the form we publish. Because our paper is being written in April 2026, we also cite SR Letter 26-2 [sr26-2], the April 2026 revision that supersedes SR 11-7 for banking organisations over the $30B asset threshold and introduces a materiality-graduated validation construct. The revision updates expectations rather than replaces them; the validation and effective-challenge requirements we target are reaffirmed.

On the backtesting methodology specifically, the Basel Committee's 1996 *Supervisory framework for the use of "backtesting"* [bcbs-backtest-1996] is the global floor. The framework defines green, yellow, and red zones for VaR backtesting based on exception counts over 250 observations, with regulatory responses escalating by zone. The mathematical content of the framework — an unconditional-coverage test over a rolling window — is the Kupiec [kupiec-1995] test we reproduce. That the same test appears in a 1995 academic paper, a 1996 supervisory framework, and a 2011 and 2026 pair of supervisory guidance letters is not an accident; it is what "institutional-grade calibration" has converged on, and it is the language we have adopted because protocol integrators and their regulators already speak it.
