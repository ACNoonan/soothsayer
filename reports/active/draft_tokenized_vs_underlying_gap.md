## §1.1.x — The tokenized-vs-underlying gap and why incumbents elide it

A categorical question precedes the integrity-versus-calibration framing of §1.1: **which price are these oracles publishing in the first place?** Two populations are simultaneously in production for tokenized US equities, and they answer different questions.

The first population publishes the **underlying ticker** — the NMS price of `SPY/USD`, `AAPL/USD`, or `TSLA/USD` itself. Pyth's core Solana surface [pyth-agg; pyth-conf], Chainlink Data Streams v10/v11's `price` field [chainlink-v10; chainlink-v11], Chainlink's EVM aggregator contracts, RedStone Live's public REST gateway [redstone-live], and Backed Finance / Ondo's issuer reference NAV all sit in this population. Each goes stale or freezes whenever NYSE is closed; Pyth Pro's Blue Ocean integration [pyth-pro; blueocean-pyth] partially extends the live window to 8 PM – 4 AM ET Sunday through Thursday, leaving the canonical Friday-16:00-ET to Sunday-20:00-ET weekend uncovered. The second population publishes the **tokenized SPL or wrapped ERC-20** — what the xStock actually trades for on crypto venues. CF Benchmarks' xStocks Indices (Kraken-owned, FCA + BMR-supervised, launched 2026-05-07), Chainlink v10/v11's `tokenizedPrice` field, every Solana DEX TWAP, and Kamino Scope's rule-based PDA all sit in this population. These feeds are continuous 24/7 by construction — their inputs are crypto-venue order books that trade through the weekend.

Neither population emits a forward-looking estimate of where the *underlying* equity will reopen with a statement of probability that the realised reopen price falls inside a given band. The structural reason is that the two populations are not solving that problem. The underlying-ticker population is solving "report the NMS print"; the tokenized-tracking population is solving "report the secondary-market price of the wrapped instrument." Both products are settlement-grade references for their respective trading venues. Neither is a risk-management primitive over the closed-market window.

### Empirical premise

Cong et al. [cong-tokenized-2025] provide the first large-scale empirical characterisation of this gap, using a hand-collected panel of the top 100 tokenized stocks issued by Backed Finance and Ondo Global Markets between July and October 2025. Their headline finding is sympathetic to tokenization on the overlapping-hours axis (Table 6, p.34): a 1% move in the underlying translates to a 0.982% move in the token, with adjusted $R^2$ of 0.982 — tokenized prices track their underliers near-one-for-one when both markets are open. **This is precisely the regime in which incumbent oracle design choices are unconstraining.** The closed-market window is different. For Tesla / TSLAx (the panel's flagship in volume and continuous-arbitrage incentive), Cong et al. document (Table 4, p.32) that the token price deviates from the last underlying close by more than 1% in **71% of weekday off-hours** and **15% of weekend hours**; by more than 2% in 57% / 8%; by more than 5% in 12% / 0%. The Ondo equivalents are 77% / 14%, 63% / 5%, 14% / 0%. These are not pathological outliers — they are the modal off-hours state of the highest-attention tokenized-equity in the panel. A single concrete weekend wedge captures the tail: TSLAx rose from $401.59 at 06:00 ET on Monday September 15, 2025 to $451.07 by 09:00 ET — a 14% premarket premium over Friday's underlying close ahead of pre-market news (Cong et al. p.24).

### Why arbitrage does not close the gap

Cong et al. attribute the persistence of off-hours deviations to the structural unavailability of the arbitrage that enforces tracking during overlapping hours [cong-tokenized-2025, §3.3.3, p.17]:

> When the underlying stock market is closed […] arbitrage constraints bind: minting and redemption may be paused, underlying liquidity is unavailable, and cross-chain fragmentation can widen effective arbitrage bands. As a result, price gaps may emerge overnight or over the weekend and persist until overlap in trading hours resumes or redemption mechanisms reactivate.

Two mechanisms reinforce this. First, redemption windows are issuer-controlled: Ondo, by stated design, "only allows redemptions during market hours to ensure they can liquidate the shares" (p.9), explicitly to prevent the off-hours fragmentation that would otherwise emerge (p.11). The issuer's own methodology acknowledges that the tokenized-side price during closed hours is informationally untethered from any redemption-anchored fundamental. Second, the marginal arbitrageur faces jurisdictional friction: Backed's xStocks are offered under European regulatory-sandbox exemptions and Ondo's product is geofenced from US individuals (p.10), so the deep US institutional arbitrage base that would otherwise close any wedge is excluded by design.

### The signal-in-mean vs. signal-in-tail dichotomy

Cong et al. provide a second, stronger result that must be honoured before the gap argument is operationalised. Off-hour token returns predict the close-to-open underlying return: in their Table 10 regression (p.40), a 1% off-hours token return predicts a **0.903% close-to-open underlying move with adjusted $R^2 = 0.839$**. This is the most explicit empirical statement in the literature that off-hour token prices contain information about where the underlying will reopen, and any version of our argument that claims tokenized prices are "noise" during closed hours contradicts it.

The dichotomy the gap framing relies on is between **conditional-mean signal** and **conditional-quantile signal**. Cong et al. demonstrate the former: a Hasbrouck-style passthrough of off-hours token returns into the underlying close-to-open conditional mean, with $\lambda = 0.903$ and one-sixth of the variance unexplained. They do not — and existing oracle methodologies do not — make any statement about the residual distribution: how often does the realised Monday open fall outside a 95%-coverage band centred on the token-implied conditional mean? The pure tokenized-tracking oracle implicitly asserts that the conditional mean *is* the answer; the underlying-frozen oracle implicitly asserts that the conditional mean equals Friday's close. Both implicit assertions are point estimates, not coverage claims. A risk-critical consumer — a lending protocol setting an LTV haircut, an options issuer pricing a strike, a regulator-facing risk model under SR 11-7 [sr11-7] / SR 26-2 [sr26-2] — needs the *tail* of the residual, not its mean. Soothsayer's contribution is to publish that tail with a Kupiec-verifiable coverage claim attached.

A regime cut sharpens the picture. Cong et al. find that off-hour token returns are *informative* about the underlying open on weekends (Table 9, p.39: weekend Q5 − Q1 token-return spread → next-day stock return of +0.77%) but **reversal-driven on weekday overnights** (Table 8, p.38: weekday-overnight Q5 − Q1 → next-day spread of −0.99%, $t \approx -2.7$). The tokenized-tracking oracle is therefore not merely uncalibrated; on weekday overnights it is *systematically biased in the wrong direction* relative to the conditional mean of the underlying. The risk-critical consumer cannot tell, from the wire output, which regime they are in.

### How each incumbent archetype elides the gap

Reading the four-tier xStock pricing plane against the conditional-quantile primitive makes the elision explicit:

1. **Underlying-frozen feeds** (Pyth core, Chainlink `price`, Backed/Ondo issuer NAV, RedStone Live SPY/QQQ/MSTR). These publish Friday's underlying close, then freeze. They contain zero off-hours information by construction and emit no width — the implicit band is degenerate at the last underlying print, which Cong et al.'s Table 4 shows is the *less-accurate* point estimate than the live tokenized side on more than half of weekday off-hours.

2. **Tokenized-tracking feeds** (CFB xStocks Indices, Chainlink `tokenizedPrice`, DEX TWAPs, Kamino Scope, Bitget RWA-hybrid, MEXC stock-perps via Ondo Global Markets). These publish the secondary-market price of the wrap, continuously, with no width on the wire. They are the on-chain implementation of the Cong et al. λ = 0.903 conditional-mean estimate — but they omit the residual variance entirely and do not distinguish the weekend-informative regime from the weekday-overnight-reversal regime.

3. **Synthetic-marker feeds** (Chainlink Data Streams v11 weekend `bid`/`ask` with `.01`-suffix at 100% incidence on three of four mapped xStocks, paper §6.7.2 [chainlink-v11]). These acknowledge the closed-market regime change in the wire output but emit a categorical bookend, not a continuous band. The receipt a consumer reads is "the closed-market regime exists" — not "the realised Monday open will lie inside the band at rate $\tau$."

4. **Forward-cursor feeds** (Pyth Pro / Blue Ocean ATS overnight book [pyth-pro; blueocean-pyth]). These extend the live underlying window into Sun–Thu nights via a real executable book, but the Friday-16:00 ET to Sunday-20:00 ET canonical weekend remains uncovered and no coverage receipt is emitted on the served value during the covered window either.

5. **Regulated point-benchmarks** (CF Benchmarks' xStocks Indices, Kraken-owned, BMR Article 27-supervised). These represent the highest methodology-disclosure rigour in the survey — input venues are governed, contributor-deviation filters are published, governance is FCA-supervised — but the wire output is a single per-second scalar $\text{CCRTI}_T$ with no confidence, band, or coverage field, and the BMR Article 27 Benchmark Statement contains no language at all about realised coverage on the served value. The most regulated reference layer in the tokenized-equity stack is silent on the conditional-quantile axis.

The categorical absence is unanimous: across regulated and unregulated, on-chain and off-chain, underlying-tracking and tokenized-tracking, point-benchmark and aggregated-band, no incumbent publishes a coverage-disclosed forward distribution on the underlying.

### Why the absence binds, and where

The absence does not affect every consumer equally. A perpetual-futures contract that is settled against its own underlying spot — Kraken's xStock perps against the CFB xStocks Indices, an Ethereum-based xStock perp marked against `tokenizedPrice` — has no economic exposure to the underlying-side gap because the contract pays out on the tokenized side. A market-making strategy on a Solana DEX consuming a TWAP-of-TWAPs is in the same position. For these consumers, soothsayer is over-engineering.

Three consumer categories are structurally exposed. **Lending protocols** that accept tokenized equities as collateral and lend USDC against them inherit the underlying-side risk, because the borrower's solvency is determined by the future redemption value of the collateral, not the secondary-market quote on a Saturday. Cong et al. note that TSLAx was added as collateral on Kamino Finance — Solana's largest lending market, with $4B in total value locked — in July 2025 (p.20). The 14% TSLAx weekend premium documented above is exactly the kind of move that forces such a market either to over-conservatively haircut against the tokenized side (a static buffer that does not adapt to regime) or to carry blind risk against the Monday-open gap. **Options or structured-product issuers** require an explicit forward distribution on the underlying to price strikes and hedge deltas; the conditional-mean passthrough Cong et al. document is necessary but not sufficient. **Regulator-facing risk models** under SR 11-7 / SR 26-2 require a documented methodology and an empirically validated coverage rate on the served value; the BMR Article 27 disclosure CFB publishes does not approach this bar by design, and an integrating bank's model-risk officer cannot defensibly substitute "we used Saturday's crypto-DEX TWAP" as a fair value for an underlying NMS instrument.

For these three categories the tokenized-vs-underlying gap is not a design preference, it is a model-risk obligation. Soothsayer occupies the unoccupied conditional-quantile-on-the-underlying slot above the existing reference layers; it does not replace any of them, and the Kupiec / Christoffersen / DQ apparatus in §6 is the disclosure form required for a risk-critical consumer to integrate any oracle on this surface at all.
