## Headline

The single largest band excursion in the published §6.x path-coverage table —
**SPY 2026-03-20 → 2026-03-23, τ = 0.68, +10.3% off the band midpoint
($648.57 anchor, band $635.99–$644.51, perp low $570)** — is a thin-liquidity
print artefact, not a fair-value event. A 0.1-unit Saturday-night trade at
$570 (notional ≈ $57) was followed by 12 continuous hours of zero-volume
bars carrying $570 forward as `close`. The realised Monday open was $658.07
(+1.46% vs Friday close), inside the band at τ = 0.99. The exemplar weekend
documented in the v1b table as the worst case did not, in fact, leave the
band on any tradable mid-price.

The same pattern holds across the rest of the top of the breach distribution.
Of the top five worst excursions in the published table:

| symbol | weekend | breach side | "extreme" price | trade behind it | volume |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| SPY | 2026-03-20 | low | 570.00 | one 0.1-unit print Sat 23:27 ET | 0.1 |
| GLD | 2026-01-30 | low | 429.10 | one 0.001-unit print Sun 22:33 ET | 0.001 |
| AAPL | 2026-02-27 | high | 274.50 | **zero trades the entire weekend** (3 931 / 3 931 no-vol bars) | 0.000 |
| MSTR | 2026-02-27 | high | 144.61 | two prints summing ≈ 3.3 units on Sun | 3.34 |
| GLD | 2026-01-16 | low | 410.40 | **zero trades the entire long weekend** (5 371 / 5 371 no-vol bars) | 0.000 |

Two of the five worst "excursions" had no trades at all — the entire weekend
is the Friday close carried forward, so the extrema are mechanically equal to
the Friday close on both legs.

5 371 for long weekends):

|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| AAPL  | 10 |  0   |  3.5 | 6 / 10  |   0.000 |
| GLD   | 19 |  1   |  8.2 | 9 / 19  |   0.002 |
| GOOGL | 10 |  0   |  7.1 | 6 / 10  |   0.000 |
| HOOD  | 10 |  8   | 35.7 | 3 / 10  | 109.760 |
| MSTR  | 10 | 13.5 | 29.3 | 0 / 10  |  86.335 |
| NVDA  | 10 |  3   | 10.8 | 1 / 10  |  48.860 |
| QQQ   | 19 | 11   | 67.8 | 2 / 19  |   5.773 |
| SPY   | 19 |  6   | 23.8 | 2 / 19  |   5.610 |
| TSLA  | 11 |  4   | 35.0 | 2 / 11  |   4.700 |

Median weekend trade share across the whole panel is **0.10%** (4 trades per
3 931 minutes); the maximum is **3.1%**. The "24/7 stock-perp 1-minute path"
language used in the v1b summary describes the *bar emission cadence*, not
the price-discovery cadence — 99.9% of the minutes carry the previous trade
forward unchanged. AAPL and GOOGL perps in particular do not, in operational
fact, price these underliers continuously over the weekend.

## Effect on §6.x aggregate numbers

Re-running path coverage with `min(low) / max(high)` over bars with
`volume_base > 0`, and dropping the 31/118 cells with zero traded bars
from the denominator (path-coverage undefined under that filter), via:

```
uv run python scripts/run_path_coverage.py --perp-volume-filter 0.0
# writes reports/tables/path_coverage_perp_traded*.csv
#        reports/v1b_path_coverage_traded.md
```

| τ | published path_cov (raw, n=118) | trade-supported path_cov (n=87) | published gap_pp | trade-supported gap_pp |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 0.68 | 34.7% | 32.2% | 29.7 | **27.6** |
| 0.85 | 56.8% | 57.5% | 29.7 | **27.6** |
| 0.95 | 78.8% | 81.6% | 16.1 | **12.6** |
| 0.99 | 91.5% | 92.0% |  7.6 | **6.9** |

Note: endpoint_cov also shifts under the filter because 31 of the 118
cells get dropped; the 87 remaining cells have a lower mean endpoint
coverage (59.8% / 85.1% / 94.3% / 98.9% at the four anchors, vs the
unfiltered 64.4% / 86.4% / 94.9% / 99.2%). The denominator change is
the dominant effect, not a change in the per-cell verdict.

Trade-supported `path_cov` lands within ~3 percentage points of the
published raw number on each side at every τ, but the underlying object
changes meaningfully: the sample drops 26% (118 → 87 cells, the 31
zero-trade weekends are removed from both numerator and denominator
because path coverage is unobservable under the trade-supported view).
The `gap_pp` headline tightens by 2–4pp across τ because both
endpoint_cov and path_cov re-shrink toward each other on the smaller
sample. **Both numbers are descriptively true** — they answer different
questions:

- **Raw `path_cov`** answers: did the perp's mark price (as a consumer
  would have read it on the exchange UI, including bar-emission carry-forward)
  ever leave the band? This is the right question for a consumer who
  liquidates against the mark print without a liquidity check.
- **Trade-supported `path_cov`** answers: did the perp's *tradable* price
  ever leave the band? This is the right question for an arbitrageur who
  must actually execute against the deviation, and for the "did fair value
  leave the band" interpretation the v1b summary leans on.

The v1b table presents only the raw number and frames it as a fair-value
path measurement.

## Recommendations for Paper 1 §6.x

These are recommendations, not commits — the cleanup belongs in the upcoming
paper-1 strategic conversation, not in WS1 Phase 2's mechanical refresh.

1. **Disclose the perp-tape liquidity.** Add a paragraph naming the 99.9%
   carry-forward share and the per-symbol traded-bar counts. The paper
   currently calls this "24/7 stock-perp 1m bars … the right object — every
   reading is what a consumer would have observed on a 24/7 reference venue
   at that minute" (§6.4); that sentence is technically true but operationally
   misleading. A reader who has not run a volume probe will believe the perp
   trades continuously.

2. **Report both filtered and unfiltered numbers.** Trade-supported
   `path_cov` is the right number for "does fair value leave the band";
   raw `path_cov` is the right number for "does a consumer's mark-driven
   liquidation decision leave the band". Both belong in the table because
   they answer different consumer-risk questions.

3. **De-emphasise the perp panel as a fair-value reference.** The CME
   factor-projected path (§6.4 sub-section 3) covers 12 years on 435
   weekends; the perp covers 19 weekends with median 0.1% trade density.
   The structural-bias risk in the factor projection (path-fitted by
   construction) is real but quantifiable; the small-sample,
   thin-liquidity risk in the perp is harder to reason about. Consider
   reweighting which one carries the headline.

4. **Pick the worked case from the trade-supported ranking.** SPY $570
   is the wrong narrative anchor. The right anchor is a weekend where
   multi-print sustained excursion happened — likely `MSTR 2026-02-27` or
   `SPY 2026-02-06` pending re-computation under the rewritten breach
   definition.

5. **Consider a microstructure footnote.** The single-print artefact at
   SPY 2026-03-20 (0.1-unit fill at $570) is itself a finding worth a line
   in the paper as a Cong-style "thin liquidity → unreliable prints"
   confirmation on the 24/7 perp side of the same question Cong asked on
   the SPL token side.
